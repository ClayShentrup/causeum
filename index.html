A matching market for political and charitable donations

I'll stop funding mine if you stop funding yours.

Alice gives $100/month to the Democrats. Bob gives $100/month to the Republicans. They're just burning money fighting each other. They could both agree to stop and donate to a worthy cause like the Against Malaria Foundation instead. Causeum does this automatically—you just state how much you value different causes, and the platform continuously finds opportunities to make your donations more efficient.

01 — How It Works

Three steps

Deposit money. It goes into a money market fund. Fully backed, always yours, withdraw anytime.

Buy cause stock. Pick the political organizations or charities you want to support. The yield your money earns flows to those causes as donations. Donations to 501(c)(3) charities are tax-deductible—we track your holdings daily so attribution is exact.

Set your prices. Rate every cause from $0.00 to $1.00—how much a dollar of funding there is worth to you. If your price is less than $1.00, that gap is what you'd need someone else to cover for you to donate there. If your price is exactly $1.00, you're saying you'd fund it entirely yourself—and your surplus is available to help bring others in.

The system continuously finds opportunities to make everyone's donations go further and executes them instantly. You get a notification when your money moves.

02 — The Core Idea

Your surplus recruits other donors

When you value a cause at $1.00, you're willing to fund every dollar yourself. But what if someone else values the same cause at $0.70? They'd donate if someone covered the $0.30 gap. Your surplus does that automatically.

You don't choose who to subsidize. You don't even know it's happening. You just state your prices and the optimizer spends your surplus where it creates the most impact—recruiting the cheapest available donors into the causes you value.

How a subsidy works

Grace values VoteVets at $1.00. She has $350 in the system. Her surplus—$0.15/dollar above what it costs to hold her own position—sits as an open offer.

Sam values VoteVets at $0.70. He'd donate there if someone covered his $0.30 gap. The optimizer sees Grace's offer and matches it with other subsidizers to cover Sam's gap.

What happens: Real money moves out of Grace's balance and into a lien on Sam's VoteVets position. Grace's redeemable balance drops. Sam holds VoteVets stock that cost him $0.70 out of pocket, with a $0.30 lien funded by Grace and others.

VoteVets now has another $1.00 of yield-generating stock. Grace spent $0.15 to help recruit it. That's leverage.

Neither party needs to know the other exists. Neither agreed to a bilateral trade. The optimizer just found that Grace's surplus and Sam's price made a match.

03 — The Lien

What keeps it locked in

When the optimizer moves someone into a subsidized position, the subsidy becomes a permanent lien on that stock. This is real money that left real accounts—not an accounting fiction.

If Sam wants to leave his VoteVets position, he repays the lien. He walks away with $0.70—exactly what he paid out of pocket. The $0.30 lien flows back to the subsidizers' pools, ready to recruit the next person.

If Grace changes her mind and lowers her VoteVets valuation, the optimizer may pull her subsidy back and the lien shrinks. Sam's out-of-pocket obligation rises. If it rises past what Sam's willing to pay, the system unwinds the position—Sam exits, Grace gets her money back.

The lien is what makes the whole system work. Without it, subsidized donors could take the cheap stock and immediately move back to their original cause—keeping the benefit without maintaining the position. The lien ensures that if you leave, you give back what you didn't pay for.

04 — The Double Effect

Defunding happens as a side effect

Grace subsidizes VoteVets because she likes VoteVets. She doesn't target anyone. But some of the people the optimizer recruits into VoteVets were previously in causes Grace hates—like Club for Growth. They left voluntarily because the subsidized price was attractive enough.

Meanwhile, Sam was independently subsidizing causes he cares about. Some of the people he recruited came from Emily's List. Neither Grace nor Sam coordinated. Neither paid the other. But the net effect is: both got their enemies defunded.

The double effect is real but emergent. You fund what you love. Defunding what you hate is a side effect of making your cause more attractive than the alternatives.

06 — Why It's Safe

No one can game this

You can only withdraw what you paid. The lien belongs to the cause until you leave, at which point it goes back to the subsidizers. There's nothing to extract.

There's no stock to resell. Anyone who wants cause stock just buys new stock at par. Cause stock has no resale value—it only has value as yield flowing to a cause you believe in.

Faking doesn't help. Parking money in a cause you hate to attract subsidies doesn't work—the subsidies are on the destination, not the origin. Cheap VoteVets stock is available to everyone regardless of what they held before. Going through Club for Growth first accomplishes nothing except bleeding yield to a cause you hate.

Moves are atomic. The optimizer executes subsidy placements atomically. You can never be left holding a position you didn't agree to.

07 — The Cobra Problem

Why most matching schemes fail—and why this doesn't

The British colonial government in India once offered a bounty for dead cobras. Locals started breeding cobras to collect the bounty. When the government cancelled the program, the breeders released their stock. More cobras than ever.

If you pay people to move money away from a cause, you incentivize people to put money into that cause to collect the payment.

You can never pay people to stop funding something. You can only make the alternatives more attractive.

Causeum avoids this because subsidies are always on the destination. You subsidize VoteVets, not "leaving Club for Growth." A faker who parks in Club for Growth hoping to collect a switching bounty finds there is no switching bounty. The cheap VoteVets stock was available to them without the detour. Meanwhile they bled yield to Club for Growth every day they were parked there.

08 — Guarantees

Properties of the system

No Extraction Possible

You can only withdraw what you paid out of pocket. Liens go back to subsidizers on exit. There's nothing to skim.

Fully Backed

Every dollar sits in a money market fund. Your out-of-pocket principal is always yours. Withdraw anytime.

Honest Pricing Is Approximately Optimal

Understating your price risks getting skipped for cheaper donors. Overstating means you pay more than the cause is worth to you. Same dynamics as any real market.

Atomic Execution

All optimizer moves execute atomically. No partial fills, no dangling positions.

Self-Correcting

When someone exits, the lien returns to the subsidizer pool and recruits the next cheapest donor. When a subsidizer changes their mind, the optimizer unwinds positions that no longer make sense.

Gets Better With Scale

More users means more surplus, more recruitable donors, and more efficient matching. The optimizer finds gains that grow with the square of the user count.

09 — Sustainability

How Causeum sustains itself

Causeum charges a modest annual management fee on total assets in the money market fund, comparable to any donor-advised fund. The incentives are clean: we grow when the fund grows.

10 — In Short

The whole idea

You buy stock in the causes you believe in. Your money earns interest that flows to those causes. You set a price for each cause. Your surplus above par automatically subsidizes other people's positions in causes you value—making those causes cheaper and attracting new donors. Subsidies become permanent liens on the stock they fund. If a recipient leaves, they get back what they paid out of pocket and the lien returns to the subsidizer. The optimizer runs continuously, finding the most efficient use of every dollar of surplus. The result: your donations go further than they would anywhere else.

Cause stock is not a security. It represents a tagged position in a money market fund whose yield flows to a designated political organization (527) or tax-exempt charity (501(c)(3)). Donations to 501(c)(3) organizations may be tax-deductible.

Step 1: Identify surplus

Grace holds EL at $1.00 and values VV at $0.85. She's happy in EL, but her surplus—the gap between her valuation and the cost of holding—is available to subsidize VV. How much surplus? She values VV at $0.85, so she'd give up $0.15/dollar to put money there. On $350, that's $52.50 of available surplus for VV subsidies.

Nora holds VV at $1.00. She has surplus available to subsidize VV too—she'd happily fund more VV. Her surplus for VV subsidies = $0.00/dollar (she's already at par). But she values EL at $0.82, so she has $0.18/dollar of surplus for EL subsidies on her $400 = $72.

Sam and Jake hold CFG at $1.00 and value VV at $0.70 and $0.60 respectively. They're potential recruits into VV if subsidized.

Step 2: The optimizer matches

The optimizer sees: $52.50 of VV subsidy available from Grace. Sam needs $0.30/dollar to switch to VV. On $300, that's $90 needed. Grace can only cover $52.50. Is there other VV surplus? Not directly—but the optimizer can search for multi-party arrangements.

Suppose Sam switches $175 of his $300 to VV (a partial switch). Cost: $0.30 × $175 = $52.50. Grace's surplus exactly covers it.

After the move:

Grace: $350 in EL, but $52.50 has been transferred as a lien onto Sam's VV position. Grace's redeemable balance: $297.50.

Sam: $125 in CFG + $175 in VV. The VV position cost him $122.50 out of pocket ($175 × 0.70). The remaining $52.50 is Grace's lien.

VV yield increased by $175/year. Grace paid $52.50 for that—about 3.3x leverage.

Step 3: What if Sam leaves?

Sam decides to sell his VV position. He repays the $52.50 lien. It flows back to Grace's surplus pool. Sam gets $122.50 back—exactly his out-of-pocket cost. Grace's redeemable balance goes back to $350. The optimizer immediately looks for the next cheapest VV recruit to place Grace's surplus with.

Step 4: What if Grace changes her mind?

Grace lowers her VV valuation to $0.50. Her surplus for VV drops. The optimizer sees that Sam's lien is no longer fully supported and unwinds the position. Sam's VV stock is sold, the lien returns to Grace, and Sam goes back to CFG. Both end up where they started. Nobody lost anything except the yield that flowed to VV during the time the position was active—which is the whole point.

Why subsidies don't need to be bilateral

Grace didn't coordinate with Sam. She didn't even know he existed. She just stated her valuations and the optimizer spent her surplus optimally. Tomorrow, Sam might leave and the optimizer recruits Jake instead (who needs $0.40/dollar—more expensive, but maybe new surplus from other users makes it viable). The system continuously rebalances as people enter, exit, and update their prices.

The double effect in practice

Grace subsidized VV. Sam moved from CFG to VV. From Grace's perspective: VV gained $175 of funding AND CFG lost $175. She didn't pay for the defunding—it happened because she made VV attractive enough that someone voluntarily left CFG. This is the double effect. It's real, it's valuable, and it's cobra-safe because Grace never referenced CFG at all.

The optimizer

The optimizer is a linear program. Variables: total dollars allocated to each cause. Objective: maximize total welfare (aggregate valuation × dollars per cause). Constraints: nobody's utility drops below their current level (Pareto), total money is conserved, and subsidy expenditures don't exceed available surplus. With 50 causes and 10,000 users, this is a ~50-variable LP with ~10,050 constraints—solvable in milliseconds by any off-the-shelf solver. It runs on every price change.

Multi-way matching

The optimizer isn't limited to one subsidizer and one recruit. It can find arrangements where Alice's surplus subsidizes VV, which recruits Bob (from CFG), whose departure frees up CFG surplus from a CFG subsidizer, which then subsidizes EL, which recruits Carol. These multi-way arrangements are found automatically by the LP—they're just properties of the optimal solution, not something the algorithm searches for explicitly.

Honest pricing

Causeum is a pay-as-bid market. Your stated price is what you pay out of pocket. Shade your bids and you risk getting skipped (if you understate) or overpaying (if you overstate). You don't know other users' positions, so gaming requires information you don't have. These are the same dynamics present in every commodity exchange and first-price auction.

The parking arbitrage

A rational actor might park money in a neutral cause, state a low VV price, and wait to be recruited at a discount. This isn't extractive—the subsidizer wanted to recruit someone, and they did. But it wastes yield (flowing to the parked cause every day) and the parker needs specific knowledge of other users' surplus to know when to park. For most users, the yield cost exceeds any strategic benefit.

Tax deductions for 501(c)(3) donations

Attribution is trivial in this model. At any moment, each user holds a specific dollar amount of each cause's stock. The yield on that amount flows to that cause. That's their donation. If the cause is a 501(c)(3), the receipt is straightforward: user X held $Y of Cause Z for N days, yield = $Y × daily rate × N. Each person's position is always unambiguously theirs. The lien doesn't complicate this—the lien holder's money is generating yield for the cause too, and they'd receive a separate receipt for that amount.

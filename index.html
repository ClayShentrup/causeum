A matching market for political and charitable donations

I'll stop funding mine if you stop funding yours.

Alice gives $100/month to the Democrats. Bob gives $100/month to the Republicans. They're just burning money fighting each other. They could both agree to stop and donate to a worthy cause like the Against Malaria Foundation instead. Causeum does this automatically—you just state how much you value different causes, and the platform continuously finds opportunities to make your donations more efficient.

01 — How It Works

Three steps

Deposit money. It goes into a money market fund. Fully backed, always yours, withdraw anytime.

Buy cause stock. Pick the political organizations or charities you want to support. The yield your money earns flows to those causes as donations. Donations to 501(c)(3) charities are tax-deductible—we track your holdings daily so attribution is exact.

Set your prices. Rate every cause from $0.00 to $1.00—how much a dollar of funding there is worth to you. These prices tell the optimizer how much you'd be willing to sacrifice to move your money to a different cause—and how much it would take to move you.

The system continuously looks for mutually beneficial trades and executes them instantly. You get a notification when your money moves.

02 — The Core Idea

Mutual compromise

Every trade on Causeum is mutual. Both sides give something up. Both sides come out ahead. The "payment" isn't cash—it's the fact that the other person also moved.

How a trade works

Grace has $350 in Emily's List. Sam has $300 in Club for Growth. They're on opposite sides. But they both moderately like VoteVets.

Emily's ListClub for GrowthVoteVets
Grace$1.00$0.10$0.85
Sam$0.10$1.00$0.90

The system moves both to VoteVets. Both sacrifice. Both gain more than they lose.

Grace's sacrifice: she left her $1.00 cause for a $0.85 cause. Cost: $0.15/dollar × $350 = $52.50 of satisfaction. Grace's gain: Sam's $300 moved from Club for Growth (worth $0.10 to her) to VoteVets (worth $0.85). Gain: $225. Net: +$172.50.

Sam's sacrifice: he left his $1.00 cause for a $0.90 cause. Cost: $0.10/dollar × $300 = $30. Sam's gain: Grace's $350 moved from Emily's List (worth $0.10 to him) to VoteVets (worth $0.90). Gain: $280. Net: +$250.

No money changed hands. They both just moved to common ground.

03 — The Lien

What each side puts down

When both sides move to a compromise, each one is sacrificing something. Grace gave up $0.15/dollar to be in VoteVets. Sam gave up $0.10/dollar. Those sacrifices are formalized as liens—real money transferred from each person's redeemable balance onto the other's position.

Grace's lien on Sam's VoteVets position represents the value she's providing by being in VoteVets instead of Emily's List. Sam's lien on Grace's position represents the same in reverse. Both positions carry liens from the other side.

If Grace wants to leave, she repays Sam's lien. She gets back her out-of-pocket cost. Sam's lien returns to him. The trade unwinds cleanly.

But the lien doesn't freeze anything. If preferences change—Grace decides she values VoteVets more, or Sam decides he values it less—the optimizer recalculates. It may shrink or grow the liens, or unwind the trade entirely. The lien is a cost in the optimization, not a lock on the position.

04 — The Double Effect

Defunding happens as a side effect

Grace didn't target Club for Growth. She just moved to VoteVets because the trade made her better off. But the result is: Club for Growth lost $300 of funding and VoteVets gained $650. Grace funded what she likes and defunded what she hates—from a single move.

Sam got the same double effect in reverse. Emily's List lost $350, VoteVets gained $650. Neither person paid the other. Neither referenced the cause they hate. The defunding is a side effect of both people finding common ground.

The double effect is real but emergent. You fund what you love. Defunding what you hate is a side effect of finding common ground with someone on the other side.

05 — The Strongest Result

Everyone is happier with where all the money ended up

When we say "no one is worse off," we don't just mean each trade made both parties happy. We mean something much stronger.

Each user's satisfaction is measured against the entire system—where every dollar sits, across all causes, including money they don't own and trades they weren't part of. If someone else's trade moved money into a cause you hate, that counts against your satisfaction. If it moved money into a cause you like, that helps you.

The optimizer only executes a batch of trades if, when you add up all the movements, every single user is at least as happy with the global outcome as they were before. Not just the trade participants. Everyone. Including bystanders.

In a 1,000-user simulation: 992 people were happier with where all the money in the system ended up—including money they don't own and trades they weren't part of. Zero people were worse off. The remaining 8 broke even.

This is possible because the optimizer sees the full picture. It won't execute a trade between Grace and Sam if doing so would make Carol worse off—even though Carol isn't involved. Carol's preference over where all money sits is a hard constraint.

The result at scale: partisan extremes collapse. The DNC, RNC, Emily's List, and NRA lost the most funding. Moderate and widely-valued causes grew. Both left-leaning and right-leaning donors saw causes they hate get defunded while causes they like gained. Both sides came out ahead because they stopped wasting money cancelling each other out.

07 — Why It's Safe

No one can game this

You can only withdraw what you paid. Both sides of a trade carry liens from the other. If you leave, you repay the lien and get back your out-of-pocket cost. There's nothing to extract.

There's no stock to resell. Anyone who wants cause stock just buys new stock at par. Cause stock has no resale value—it only has value as yield flowing to a cause you believe in.

Faking doesn't help. Parking money in a cause you hate hoping to be a more attractive trading partner just bleeds yield to that cause every day you hold it. And since the optimizer matches based on everyone's stated prices, you'd need specific knowledge of other users' positions to game anything—information you don't have.

Bystanders are protected. The optimizer won't execute trades that make non-participants worse off. Your satisfaction with the global allocation is a hard constraint.

08 — The Cobra Problem

Why most matching schemes fail—and why this doesn't

The British colonial government in India once offered a bounty for dead cobras. Locals started breeding cobras to collect the bounty. When the government cancelled the program, the breeders released their stock. More cobras than ever.

If you pay people to move money away from a cause, you incentivize people to put money into that cause to collect the payment.

You can never pay people to stop funding something. You can only give them a reason to move somewhere they like better.

Causeum avoids this because there are no bounties. Every trade is a mutual compromise—both sides move to common ground and both carry liens. Nobody gets paid to leave a cause. A faker who parks money in a hated cause bleeds yield to that cause while waiting for a trade that never references where anyone came from.

09 — Guarantees

Properties of the system

No Extraction Possible

Both sides carry liens. You can only withdraw your out-of-pocket cost. There's nothing to skim.

Fully Backed

Every dollar sits in a money market fund. Your out-of-pocket principal is always yours. Withdraw anytime.

Bystanders Protected

The optimizer won't execute trades that make non-participants worse off. Everyone's satisfaction with the global allocation is a constraint.

Atomic Execution

All trades execute atomically. Both sides move together or neither does.

Honest Pricing Is Approximately Optimal

Misrepresenting your values risks ending up in causes you don't like or missing trades that would benefit you. Same dynamics as any real market.

Gets Better With Scale

More users means more ideological diversity, more common ground to find, and bigger gains. The optimizer finds gains that grow with the square of the user count.

10 — Sustainability

How Causeum sustains itself

Causeum charges a modest annual management fee on total assets in the money market fund, comparable to any donor-advised fund. The incentives are clean: we grow when the fund grows.

11 — In Short

The whole idea

You buy stock in the causes you believe in. Your money earns interest that flows to those causes. You set a price for each cause. The optimizer continuously finds pairs or groups of donors who are funding each other's enemies and routes them to common ground. Both sides give something up. Both sides gain more than they lose. Both carry liens from the other to prevent reversal. Bystanders are protected—no trade executes if it makes anyone in the system worse off. Your principal is always yours. And if the common ground is a charity, you get a tax deduction.

Cause stock is not a security. It represents a tagged position in a money market fund whose yield flows to a designated political organization (527) or tax-exempt charity (501(c)(3)). Donations to 501(c)(3) organizations may be tax-deductible.

Round 2: Preferences change

A week later:

1. Rosa changes her mind. She lowers LCV from $1.00 to $0.50 and raises AMF to $0.90.

2. Tina joins with $400, valuing LCV at $0.95.

3. Sam raises his VV valuation from $0.70 to $0.90.

The optimizer re-runs. Rosa's liens on Omar's LCV position are no longer justified at her new valuation—the cost outweighs the benefit. The optimizer unwinds them: Rosa's money returns to her balance, Omar's position loses the subsidy. The optimizer then looks for new trades with Rosa's freed-up capital (now aimed at AMF) and Tina's fresh money (aimed at LCV). Sam's stronger VV preference means his existing trade is more stable—less lien needed from Nora.

The system reshuffles, creating new mutual compromises that reflect everyone's current preferences. Some old liens dissolve. New ones form. Money flows to wherever the math says it does the most good.

The key lesson

Liens aren't locks. They're living costs in a continuously re-optimizing system. Every trade is mutual—both sides sacrifice, both gain, both carry liens from the other. When preferences change or new users arrive, the optimizer reshuffles everything.

Left-leaning donors saw $62,541 flow toward their causes and $57,574 defunded from the right. Right-leaning donors saw $33,365 toward theirs and $53,115 defunded from the left. Both sides came out ahead. Centrist/charity causes grew 164%.

And remember—"happier" means happier with where ALL the money sits, not just their own. Every bystander's preferences were respected.

Technical notes

The optimizer is a linear program. Variables: total dollars per cause. Objective: maximize total welfare. Constraints: every user's satisfaction with the global allocation must not decrease, total money conserved. ~50 variables, ~10,050 constraints at scale. Solvable in milliseconds. Runs on every preference change.

Multi-way matching. The optimizer finds n-way cycles (Shapley-Scarf Top Trading Cycles) where no pairwise trade exists. These are properties of the LP solution, not a separate search.

Honest pricing is approximately optimal. Causeum is a pay-as-bid market with standard bid-shading dynamics—same as any commodity exchange.

The parking arbitrage. Parking money in a neutral cause wastes yield and requires information about other users you don't have. For most users, not worth it.

Tax deductions. Each user holds a specific dollar amount of each cause's stock at any moment. Yield = balance × rate × days. If the cause is a 501(c)(3), that's the receipt. Lien holders get a separate receipt.
